Research Interests

As part of the undergraduate research apprentice program (URAP) at UC Berkeley, I joined the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) project with Dr. Allyson Brodzeller and the Lawrence Berkeley National Lab (LBNL).

I currently assist in developing a method to classify damped Lyman-alpha absorbers (DLAs). These objects absorb emissions from quasars on our light of sight. Specifically, we look at the lyman-alpha line, which is a hydrogen line caused by the n=2 to the n=1 ground state. This occurs at a restframe wavelength of 1215.67 angstroms. DLAs consist of lots of neutral hydrogen and when the neutral hydrogen column density reaches around \(10^{20.3} cm^{-2}\). This is quite important because they can tell us about our cosmological history. Directly, DLAs can tell us about Baryonic Acoustic Oscillations (BAOs) which tell us about the evolution and history of matter in our universe. Indirectly, these objects actually contaminate our detection of distant objects like quasars. By properly modeling and classifying DLAs, we can decontaminate quasars and other sightlines, allowing us to better measure other quantities of the universe.
